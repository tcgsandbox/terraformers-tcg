# Why the Desert Sleeps

The mortals who walk the dune-paths will tell you that the desert is dead. They are wrong. The desert is only resting.

There was once a god — a small one, quick and clever — who tried to make a land that produced without pause. She gathered the brightest sand she could find, threaded it through with veins of gold, and breathed into it a hunger for the sun. For one perfect day, her land gave more tribute than any other on the planet. Mortals came from every direction to live upon it. Crops grew overnight. Cities rose by sundown.

By morning, the land was empty.

The little god, embarrassed, tried to start again. She breathed harder this time. The same thing happened. Whatever she made, the world consumed in a single day.

Finally, an older god — slow, with mountain-grey eyes — sat down beside her and laid a hand on the sand.

*"You cannot give and give,"* he said. *"You must rest, or you will be eaten."*

So the little god taught her land to sleep. One day to give, one day to gather itself. Two cycles, forever, in turn.

This is why, even now, the desert produces a great bounty — and then is still. This is why the gods who covet the desert must also covet patience. And this is why the older gods watch their younger rivals with such quiet amusement: they remember being young, and they remember learning the same lesson.
